## The Lifespan Human Connectome Project in Development: A large-scale study of brain connectivity development in 5–21 year olds

**abstract:**

Examination of brain organization and connectivity. HCP-D exploiting tools to chart changes in brain connectivity.

* When complete, the HCP-D will comprise 
  * approximately ~1750 open access datasets 
    * from 1300+ healthy participants, 
      * ages 5-21 years, 
      * acquired at four sites across the USA.
      * The participants are from diverse geographical, ethnic, and socioeconomic background. 
      * While most participants are tested once, others take part in a three-wave longitudinal component focused on the pubertal period (ages 9-17 years).
    * Brain imaging sessions are acquied on a 3 T Siemens Prisma platform and include 
      * structural, functional (resting state and task-based), diffusion, and perfusion imaging,
      *  physiological monitoring,
      *  and a battery of cognitive tasks and self-reports.
    * For minors, parents additionally complete a battery of instruments to characterize 
      * cognitive and emotional development, 
      * and environmrntal variables relevant to development.
    * Participants provide biological samples of 
      * blood, 
      * saliva, 
      * and hair, 
    * enabling assays of 
      * pubertal hormones, 
      * health markers, 
      * and banked DNA samples.

This paper outlines the overarching aims of the projects, the approach taken to acquire maximally informative data while minimizing participant burden, preliminary analyses, and discussion of the intended uses and limitations of the dataset.

**1.Overview OF HCP-D**

***1.1. Aims***

1. Adape existing HCP protocals to the practical challenges of studying developmental populations.
2. Acquire high quality multimodal imaging data to characterize age-related changes in brain network organization and connectivity.
3. Prioritize infection points of health-relevant behavioral changes with specific developmental phases.
4. Optimize data processing schemes for developmental data and make the data and analytic tools publicly available for the scientific community.

***1.2. Relation to other brain imaging projects***

The HCP-D builds on the success of the HCP Young Adult (HCP-YA) project

* studied 1100 22-35 year olds from 2010 to 2016 (Van Essen et al., 2013)

Continuity across the lifespan is provided by:

* The Human Connectome Project in Aging (HCP-A)
  * spans ages 36-100 + years (Bookheimer et al., under review)
  * and the "Baby Connectome" project spans ages 0-5 years using methods customized for very young children (https://www.med.unc.edu/bric/ideagroup/)

Synergy with other larger-scale iamging srudies on developmental populations:

* the Philadelphia Neurodevelopmental Cohort (Satterthwaite et al., 2014a; age 8-21 years),
* the Pediatric Imaging, Neurocognition, and Genetics study (Brown et al., 2012; age 3-20 years),
* Imagen (Schumann et al., age 14-16 years),
* and Generation R (Kooijman et al., 2016, White et al., 2018; age 6-11 years)

HCP-D is also complementary to

* the ongoing Adolelscent Brain and Cognitive Development (ABCD) project (Volkow et al., 2017; Casey et al., 2018).

Mainly a cross-sectional project spanning ages 5-21 years, with embedded longitudinal cohorts around puberty.
